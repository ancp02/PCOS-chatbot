Infertility is defined as the failure to achieve pregnancy after 12 months or more of regular unprotected sexual intercourse. —WHO

Approximately one in six people of reproductive age worldwide experience infertility in their lifetime. —WHO

Infertility can be primary, where no prior pregnancy has been achieved, or secondary, where difficulty conceiving occurs after a previous pregnancy. —WHO

Female infertility causes include tubal disorders (such as blocked fallopian tubes from untreated infections), uterine disorders (like fibroids or endometriosis), ovarian disorders including PCOS, and hormonal/endocrine abnormalities. —WHO

Male infertility is often caused by obstruction in the reproductive tract, hormonal imbalances affecting testosterone production, testicular failure, and abnormal sperm function or morphology. —WHO

Lifestyle factors such as smoking, excessive alcohol, obesity, and exposure to environmental toxins negatively affect fertility in both men and women. —WHO

Equitable access to fertility care, including diagnosis and treatment, remains limited globally, especially in low- and middle-income countries. —WHO

Treatments for infertility include prevention, early diagnosis, medical therapy, and assisted reproductive technologies such as in vitro fertilization (IVF). —WHO

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a common hormonal disorder affecting ovarian function, characterized by at least two of the following: irregular ovulation, elevated androgen levels, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. —NHS / WHO

PCOS affects about 1 in 10 women and often presents in late teens or early adulthood. —NHS

Polycystic ovaries contain numerous small follicles that fail to regularly release eggs, causing ovulation problems. —NHS

Symptoms of PCOS include irregular or absent periods, infertility due to anovulation, excessive hair growth (hirsutism), weight gain, thinning scalp hair, acne, oily skin, and skin tags. —NHS / Cleveland Clinic

PCOS increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes, high cholesterol, hypertension, sleep apnea, depression, and endometrial cancer if untreated. —NHS / Cleveland Clinic

The exact cause of PCOS is unknown but involves genetic predisposition, insulin resistance, and hormonal imbalances including high androgens and luteinising hormone (LH). —NIH / NHS

Insulin resistance causes elevated insulin levels that stimulate excess androgen production, disrupting follicle development and ovulation. —NIH / NHS

Obesity worsens insulin resistance and androgen excess, exacerbating PCOS symptoms. —NHS

PCOS is diagnosed when at least two of the following criteria are present after excluding other causes: irregular menstrual cycles, clinical or biochemical signs of hyperandrogenism, and polycystic ovaries on ultrasound. —NHS / Cleveland Clinic

Blood tests for hormone levels, glucose, and lipids, along with pelvic ultrasound, are used in diagnosis and monitoring. —NHS

There is no cure for PCOS, but management includes lifestyle modifications such as weight loss, a healthy diet, and exercise. —NHS / Cleveland Clinic

Medications for PCOS symptoms include hormonal contraceptives to regulate periods and reduce androgen effects, ovulation inducers like clomiphene and letrozole, and insulin-sensitizing agents such as metformin. —NHS / Cleveland Clinic

For hirsutism and acne, treatments include combined oral contraceptives, anti-androgens (spironolactone, cyproterone acetate), eflornithine cream, and cosmetic hair removal methods. —NHS

If medications fail to induce ovulation, laparoscopic ovarian drilling may be performed to restore ovulation by reducing androgen production. —NHS

Women with PCOS have a higher risk of pregnancy complications including gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, miscarriage, and preterm birth, particularly if overweight or obese. —Cleveland Clinic

Many women with PCOS can conceive naturally or with fertility treatments, including assisted reproductive technologies like IVF. —NHS / Cleveland Clinic

Effective infertility management requires early diagnosis, personalized treatment, and addressing lifestyle factors to improve fertility and reduce health risks. —WHO
